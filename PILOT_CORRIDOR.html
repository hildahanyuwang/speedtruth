Pilot intervention dossier, Katraj–Dehu Bypass, Pune

A worked, decision-ready example of a SpeedTruth Priority-1 corridor: what is wrong, what the evidence shows, what to do, and what it returns. ADB AI for Safer Roads 2026 · Maharashtra (India)

1. The corridor

  • Where: Katraj–Dehu Road Bypass, western edge of Pune, Maharashtra (≈18.45–18.69 N, 73.72–73.89 E), a ~52 km arterial bypass carrying through-traffic around the city.
  • What SpeedTruth flags: 10 of its 14 assessed segments are Priority 1 (2 Priority 2); 3 are absolutely Critical and 7 Serious on the Safe-System severity scale.

2. The misalignment, the limit, not the driver

  • The corridor is posted at highway speed though it functions as an urban bypass. Operating speeds run below the posted limit, drivers are not the problem; the limit itself is set for a free-flowing highway on a road that no longer behaves like one, so enforcement alone cannot fix it.
  • The road carries dense vulnerable mixed traffic: auto-rickshaws, motorcycles (helmet-wearing only ~24% in urban Maharashtra), pedestrians and trucks sharing largely undivided carriageway with no footpaths. The Safe-System survivable speed for this mix is 30–50 km/h (WHO), well below the posted limit. (Specific per-segment posted limits and probe speeds are confidential under the challenge data agreement and are omitted here; the classification, diagnosis and recommendation below are derived outputs.)
  • This is a governance-maintenance failure: a bypass posted at highway speed that urban development and informal traffic have since grown into, without the limit being revised.

3. The evidence (this is not inferred, it is photographed)

  • Street-view review of four points on the corridor directly shows the conditions (STREETVIEW_VERIFICATION.md; images output/blindtest_images/img_01,10,17,21.jpg): an auto-rickshaw mid-carriageway among motorcycles, a truck and pedestrians, parked two-wheelers, roadside vendors, dust verges and no pedestrian footpath, on a road posted at highway speed.
  • These are exactly the “invisible fleet” users (informal three-wheelers, unhelmeted riders) that probe-speed data cannot see, which is why the operating-speed signal alone understates the risk here.

4. Recommended intervention (aligned with GRSF “what works”)

Tier Action Cost Evidence
Immediate Reduce posted limit to 50 km/h (built-up sections) and 30 km/h at the 5 school/market segments; re-sign Near-zero WHO Safe System; a limit re-set is the cheapest credible measure
Medium Enforcement + traffic-calming where operating speed still exceeds the new limit Medium GRSF
Structural Separated footways, safe crossings, and motorcycle lanes where the road must stay fast Higher GRSF: footways −40–60% pedestrian casualties

5. What it returns

  • The matching iRAP Indian deployments return benefit–cost ratios of ~13–14 for pedestrian footpaths and crossings (iRAP India Four States, 2011), the intervention classes recommended here.
  • Because the binding constraint is the posted limit, the first step is a near-zero-cost quick win: re-signing the corridor to a survivable speed delivers immediate risk reduction before any capital works.
  • (Honest: with no segment-level crash counts this is a Safe-System and Power-Model case, not a casualty forecast; see FINDINGS.md §4, §6.)

6. Why this corridor first

It combines everything ADB prioritises: a motorcycle/auto-rickshaw corridor with very low helmet use, school and market exposure, photographed vulnerable mixing, and a limit that is the lever (operating speed is already below it, so enforcement alone cannot fix it). It is a textbook case where correcting the limit, not chasing the driver, saves lives, and where SpeedTruth turns a network-wide scan into a single, costed, actionable decision.
